Electrical Resistivity & Vertical Electrical Sounding (VES) in Corpus Christi

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

LEARN MORE →

The most common mistake we see on the Texas Gulf Coast is assuming uniform soil conditions between two boreholes. In Corpus Christi, the Beaumont Formation clays can mask deep paleochannels or saltwater wedges that a standard drill rig simply misses. We run Vertical Electrical Sounding to map these contrasts before the foundation design is locked in. The array measures apparent resistivity at increasing electrode spacings, building a 1D geoelectric profile directly below the survey point. For coastal sites near the ship channel or the Laguna Madre, the contrast between fresh groundwater lenses and intruding saline water shows up clearly in the resistivity curve. We often combine the VES data with CPT soundings to calibrate the resistivity layers against measured pore pressure and tip resistance, giving the geotechnical engineer a continuous subsurface model instead of isolated data points.

Resistivity is not an abstract geophysical parameter here. It directly tracks pore fluid salinity and clay content, the two variables that control foundation performance on the Texas Coastal Plain.

Methodology and scope

In the Corpus Christi area, we see a lot of surface cracking in commercial slabs traced back to differential settlement over relict oxbow deposits. The VES method catches these features because the clay-filled channels and the surrounding Pleistocene terrace sands have a resistivity contrast of an order of magnitude. Our field crew uses a Schlumberger array with maximum current electrode spacing up to 600 feet, which gets us penetration depths of roughly 100 to 150 feet depending on the local stratigraphy. We record the apparent resistivity at each expansion step and invert the curve using a layered-earth algorithm. The output is not a fuzzy image. It is a quantitative table of layer resistivities and thicknesses. For projects where the client needs lateral coverage as well, we deploy a multi-electrode resistivity profile inline with the VES stations. The methodology aligns with ASTM D6431 for surface geophysical methods. On sites where the upper 10 feet are critical for pavement design, we also recommend a CBR road subgrade assessment to tie the geophysical data to a direct bearing capacity value.

Local geotechnical context

The Beaumont Formation clays that underlie most of Corpus Christi are notoriously expansive and have a high cation exchange capacity. When saline water from the Nueces Bay or the ship channel intrudes into a freshwater clay layer, the pore fluid chemistry changes, and the double-layer thickness collapses. The soil loses strength without any visible change in texture. A resistivity survey detects this shift because the bulk resistivity drops from above 5 ohm-m to below 1 ohm-m. We have mapped saline intrusion plumes migrating over 800 feet inland from the shoreline at depths of 40 to 60 feet. Ignoring this mechanism leads to long-term settlement that no amount of surcharge preloading will fix. The other risk in this region is paleokarst in the deeper limestone formations. A sharp resistivity high at the base of a VES sounding, especially below 100 feet, often indicates a cavernous zone that can cause sudden drill fluid loss during piling. We flag these anomalies before a rig ever mobilizes.

Questions and answers

What is the typical cost of a Vertical Electrical Sounding survey in Corpus Christi?
Can resistivity methods detect the depth to the Beaumont Formation base?

Yes, that is one of the main applications here. The Beaumont clays are typically conductive, below 10 ohm-m, while the underlying Pleistocene sands and the Goliad Formation show resistivities above 30 ohm-m. The VES curve picks up this transition reliably between 60 and 120 feet across most of Nueces County.

How does saltwater from the bay affect the resistivity readings?

Saltwater is an excellent conductor. Pore fluid with total dissolved solids above 10,000 mg/L drops the bulk resistivity of the formation below 1 ohm-m. We use this property to map the freshwater-saltwater interface. The VES curve will show a sharp resistivity decrease at the depth where saline intrusion begins, which is critical for any project involving deep excavations or dewatering near the ship channel.
